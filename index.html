While my game development journey is still young, I’ve worked on these projects that I’m excited to share with you.

Dark Hike

Dark Hike was a student collaboration between programmers and artists, aiming to blend the atmosphere of Dark Souls with the lighthearted exploration of A Short Hike. The story follows a knight who awakens in the Plane between life and death. The result? You be the judge. Try the playable demo on itch.io.

My role: I was responsible for implementing the player movement system and programming interactions in Unreal Engine using C++. I also took the lead on designing and integrating the narrative elements of the game, shaping the story and dialogue to match the game's atmospheric tone.

Key takeaways from the project:

  • Programming: First hands-on experience with Unreal Engine and C++
  • Project Management: Coordinated a large, multidisciplinary team of 14
  • Game Design: Learned the importance of a clear, unified design vision

This was my first time working with Unreal Engine, and a significant part of the development was spent learning its workflow and best practices. None of the programmers had prior Unreal experience, so we started from scratch and learned by doing.

The team included 3 programmers, 9 artists, 2 sound designers, and a marketing student as the project manager. Despite its size, the team functioned efficiently. We faced some expected friction between disciplines, but overall, the collaboration was smooth and respectful.

The biggest challenge was the game design. Our initial concept lacked a strong, cohesive vision, which led to design inconsistencies. We originally aimed for minimal mechanics —just walking and exploration— but eventually pushed through to include more interactive elements. Several great ideas were left on the cutting room floor, and with them, the potential for a truly unique experience.

In the end, I’m proud of what we accomplished. While not perfect, the game looks fantastic and was a valuable learning experience in both technical and collaborative aspects.

Check out the GitHub repo and watch the gameplay video. You can also play the prototype on itch.io.

ChompTown

ChompTown was our team’s entry for Global Game Jam 2024, where the theme was “Make Me Laugh.” It’s a 3D action game where you play as an escaped crocodile rampaging through a mountain village, eating its quirky inhabitants so you don’t starve.

Beyond the 48-hour time crunch, our biggest challenge was coordinating a 12-person team. But we pulled it off — and had a blast doing it!

My role: I was responsible for designing and balancing the core gameplay systems (scoring, eating mechanic, hunger gauge), programming the physics-based destruction of the environment, and creating all the in-game sound effects using only my voice — in line with the jam’s “Mouthing Off” diversifier.

Key takeaways from the project:

  • Working with a large team in a fast-paced jam setting taught me how to manage time, scope, and communication efficiently.
  • System design needs to feel intuitive and rewarding — balancing mechanics like the hunger meter made a big impact on the gameplay loop.
  • Fun physics interactions can make a game feel lively and satisfying, but performance considerations matter, especially when dealing with destructible environments.
  • Audio can make or break the comedic tone — even improvised sounds from your own mouth can land well if they fit the theme!

The goal of this project was just to have fun with old and new friends and do some funny stuff with the game jam theme. And I think that's exactly what we did. The only troubles we had were synchronizing the team of 12 people and having a game up and running in just 48 hours. Otherwise, we had a great time messing around with ideas.

It didn't come out as polished as I would've liked, but it turned out great. We even went on to showcase the game at the IGDA Helsinki meetup in February 2024, where it received a positive reception!

Escape from Caduceus

Escape from Caduceus is a VR puzzle demo I created as a solo project for a course assignment in my VR/AR/Console Development course. This was my first experience with both VR and proper 3D development. The game is set on a space station with lost gravity, where players solve puzzles while navigating a zero-gravity environment in VR.

My role: I handled every aspect of development — from game and level design to programming, visuals, and testing.

Key takeaways from the project:

  • Level Design: Learned how to design interactive spaces that work well in both 3D and VR
  • Programming: Built custom systems for movement and interaction that minimized motion sickness in a zero-gravity setting
  • VR-Specific Design: Discovered how critical comfort and immersion are to VR experiences — especially in terms of movement and lighting

Since this was my first take on 3D and VR development, I learned a lot — especially how different VR is from traditional 3D game design. The third dimension wasn’t as tricky as I expected, but VR definitely introduced new challenges. One major focus was reducing motion sickness in a zero-gravity environment, which I think I managed well.

The look and feel of the game were especially challenging. Lighting and rendering quality have a huge impact in VR, and I still have more to learn in that area — but I was proud of the final result, even if it wasn’t perfect.

Designing for VR turned out to be the most demanding but also the most fun part. Exploring the possibilities of immersive interaction was genuinely exciting, and this project made me want to keep going with VR development.

Invasion of Space Molluscs

Invasion of Space Molluscs was my second school project — a solo Unity game inspired by classic arcade shooters. The assignment was to pick an old-school arcade game and develop a clone in Unity. I chose to take on the entire development process myself as a challenge.

Key areas of focus:

  • Programming: Deepened my C# knowledge in Unity and explored new techniques
  • Game Graphics: Created all the sprites, animations, and destructible pixel environments
  • Game Audio: Composed chiptune-style background music and sound effects
  • Game Design: Adapted and refined the gameplay of a classic arcade title

Handling all aspects of development was rewarding but also a little lonely. I missed the creative energy that comes from working with a team. One of the hardest parts was creating audio from scratch — I had no prior experience with sound design. I taught myself to use BeepComp, a tool for composing 8-bit Nintendo-style music and effects, which reminded me of old-school mobile ringtone creation.

Another technical challenge was implementing destructible shields, which required pixel-level collision detection. It took some trial and error, but I figured it out and felt proud to have solved it on my own.

This project taught me a lot — from creating destructible environments to implementing save/load systems, and most importantly, how to manage a full game project solo from start to finish.

Chess Bot Project

Chess Bot Project is a classic human vs AI text-based chess game — and it marked my first experience developing a game in C++. It was created as a course assignment in a team of four programmers.

My role: I integrated multithreading to improve AI response times, optimized algorithms for better performance, particularly in handling recursive functions, and led the testing and debugging efforts to ensure smooth gameplay.

Key programming lessons and skills:

  • C++ Programming: Strengthened my skills in a new language
  • AI Development: Tackled moderately complex AI logic for chess
  • Multithreading: Implemented multithreaded logic to speed up the bot's decision-making
  • Performance Optimization: Fine-tuned algorithms by minimizing unnecessary variable copying
  • Debugging & Testing: Spent a lot of time testing and debugging critical game logic

The project began by familiarizing ourselves with a base code provided by the instructor. Understanding and building upon legacy code helped me gain confidence in reading and extending someone else’s work. We then implemented the full game logic based on the rules of chess — a process that required careful testing and precision.

My favorite part was designing and optimizing the AI. I experimented with different data structures and recursive algorithms, paying special attention to performance. By reducing unnecessary variable copying, I significantly boosted the efficiency of the decision-making process. I also managed to integrate multithreading into the bot’s logic, which greatly improved response times.

At the end of the course, all student teams competed in a chess bot tournament. Our bot performed well — losing only to one other team — which was a rewarding payoff for our hard work.

This was the project where I grew the most as a programmer. It was a deep dive into coding, algorithms, data structures, and performance optimization — and it left me with a stronger grasp of C++ and a passion for AI programming.

Check out the GitHub repo to see the code — and feel free to download the bot and challenge it yourself!

Under the Veil of Darkness

Under the Veil of Darkness was the first larger school project developed in a team. It resulted in a game prototype for the client game studio, Nitro Games, aimed at experimenting with new game mechanics for FPS games. The prototype was later published on itch.io.

My Role: I took over leadership due to challenges in team dynamics, focused on game mechanics and balance despite limited time and contributed to designing the Preset Management System.

The key feature of the game was the Preset Management System, which allowed players to assign points to five different attributes: Sight, Hearing, Movement, Attack, and Defense. Players could divide 10 points across these attributes, with one attribute maxed out at 4 points, and create two different presets for switching during gameplay based on the situation.

What I learned:

  • Programming: Learned to program a FPS and 3D game from scratch
  • Project Management: Managed a team with varying levels of motivation and skill
  • Game Design: Learned how challenging it can be to balance game mechanics effectively

This was my first project in FPS game development, and it took some time to figure out where to focus our efforts. Luckily, we had the opportunity to attend a presentation by Nitro Games, which helped us understand the importance of proper code architecture for larger games. We adopted the service locator design pattern after learning about it — a step up from the singleton pattern I had used before, and it was relatively easy to understand and implement.

The project also introduced me to a challenging team dynamic. The lack of motivation and differing work schedules made it hard to find a common rhythm. To address this, I took on the team lead role and tried to keep the team on track through daily meetings and setting sprint goals using Trello. Despite some communication breakdowns, I had to step in with an intervention after a particularly unproductive week, which temporarily helped improve our performance.

The main difficulty, however, was in balancing the gameplay. We spent the first three weeks on concept creation, which meant that actual programming didn’t start until week 4. By the time we began coding, I realized that we should have focused on a minimum viable product right away and prioritized gameplay balancing. Unfortunately, we lost focus on the critical path, and as a result, most of the balancing was rushed in the final two days due to time constraints and a lack of work ethic within the team.

Even though the project didn’t meet all of my expectations, I’m still proud of the experience because of the invaluable lessons I learned along the way.
